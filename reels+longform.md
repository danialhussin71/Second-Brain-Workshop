2. GOLDEN RULES (the physics of short-form)
1. The 3-second gate. ~63% of the highest-CTR videos hook viewers within 3
seconds; the platform decision window is ~1.5s. Benchmarks: 70% retention at≥
3s = promotable; 85% = viral potential; <60% = dead.≥
2. Triple-channel hook. Every open fires three hooks at once:
○ Visual hook — the first frame communicates the premise with sound OFF (start
mid-action, before/after contrast, prop in hand).
○ Verbal hook — first spoken line, 8-12 words, delivered in ~2-2.5 seconds.≤
○ Text hook — on-screen headline that ADDS to the spoken line
(stakes/contradiction), never transcribes it.
3. Words-per-second math. ~2.2-2.8 w/s. If the script is 100 words, the video is ~40-45
seconds. Cut words, never rush delivery.
4. The "no intro" rule. No logo, no title card, no name, no "hey guys". Replace intro with a
one-line foreshadow: what's coming + the conflict + what they get ("By the end you'll
know the exact 3 words that doubled my reply rate — but the second one feels wrong").
5. But/Therefore (South Park rule). ~70% "but" (tension), 30% "therefore"
(consequence). "And then" = list, not story.
6. Value-per-second. Every sentence advances the story or pays something off. Test
each line: "would anyone rewind for this?" If not, delete. (Hoyos: removing even ONE
second measurably improves performance.)
7. Visual change every 2-3 seconds. Any 5-second block with no cut/zoom/text/SFX is a
retention leak.
8. Duration strategy. A 22s Short watched twice beats a 45s Short watched once.
Sweet spots: ~34s for high-retention (Hoyos targets 90% retention there); 60-75s≥
only if you can hold 80%+ completion.
9. End = emotional memory. Viewers judge the whole video by the intensity of the ending.
"Leave them satisfied, but surprised." Write the last line to feed the first (seamless loop).
10. One video = one idea. One takeaway, one promise, one payoff.

3.4 Hook writing rules
● Hooks must sound SPOKEN, not like headlines. Test: would you say it out loud to a
friend? ("Okay so I just found out…" beats "The Ultimate Guide To…")
● 20 words, ideally 12.≤ ≤
● Specificity beats cleverness: "$1 chicken sandwich" beats "making cheap food".
Power words with built-in stakes: banned, free, one dollar, secret, cheap, illegal,
exposed.
● Hormozi's 3 verbal archetypes: shock stat · provocative question · bold promise. His 5
hook sources: far past, recent past, present moment, trends, manufactured (crazy
ideas).
● Brendan Kane: a Hook Point = specificity + intrigue. First 3 seconds must be
satisfying, correctly paced, linear.
● Be willing to change the entire video idea for a stronger hook (Hoyos).
● Generate 5+ hook options per topic, score each 1-100 on scroll-stop strength, clarity,
specificity. Ship the winner.
● Never write a hook the body can't cash. Hook-bait mismatch spikes 3s retention but
tanks completion and trains the algorithm that you disappoint.

5. CTA LIBRARY
5.1 Why hard CTAs hurt
A 3-second spoken "follow for more" on a 20s Short is 15% of runtime with zero value, and it
breaks the loop. Terminal CTAs sacrifice the replay signal. Default: hard CTA in the
caption/pinned comment; spoken CTA soft, specific, value-tied.
5.2 By funnel stage (from the Script-Craft CTA library)
TOFU (awareness / engagement):
● "Comment if you've experienced this."
● "Tag someone who needs to see this."
● "Save this for later."
● "Send this to someone who ___." (Reels superpower — saves/sends weigh heavily)
MOFU (consideration / value exchange):
● "Comment '[WORD]' and I'll send you the [resource]." (only if you actually deliver)
● "Follow for Part 2 tomorrow." (tie the follow to a specific future payoff)
● "Follow — I post one of these every day at 7."
BOFU (conversion):
● "Link in bio to book a free call."
● "DM me 'READY'."
● "Only [X] spots left."
5.3 The loop-closing CTA (strongest for Shorts)
Write the ending so the last line/frame flows into the first: "…which is exactly why I started with
this." The rewatch IS the CTA.

DO

● Colin & Samir's 3 rules: (1) if they don't click, they don't watch; (2) respect their time —

3. INTRO FORMULAS (0:00–0:30)
Length: branded/logo intros 5s or cut entirely. The scripted intro completes its work by≤
15-30s. ~55% of viewers are gone by 60s when the intro is weak.
Formula A — Cold Open
Start mid-action/mid-consequence, zero context; show the most dramatic moment (or its
precursor), then rewind. Rule: the cold open should be a clip the thumbnail could have been
taken from.
Formula B — PPP (Preview / Proof / Roadmap) — default for educational
1. Preview (0-10s): restate the title promise concretely — "By the end of this video you'll
have X."
2. Proof (10-20s): credibility receipt on screen — "I used this to do Y."
3. Roadmap (20-30s): the 3-5 things coming, framed as open loops, best teased last —
"…and #4 is the one nobody talks about."
(Variant PVSS adds explicit Stakes: what you risk by not watching.)
Formula C — Context / Stakes / Payoff-Promise — for narrative & essays
1. Context: who/where/what in 1-2 sentences over a strong visual anchor (Johnny Harris:
a piece of visual evidence that communicates without narration, placed in the first 30-
60s).
2. Stakes: why it matters, why now. George Blackman's hook triad — Target,
Transformation, Stakes (education) or Character, Concept, Stakes (entertainment).
Don't optimize hook length; make every word justify itself.
3. Payoff promise: open the "grand loop" — hint at the destination without revealing it.
Formula D — Misconception-First (Veritasium)
Open on a counterintuitive question or someone confidently giving the WRONG answer.
Muller's PhD finding: clear facts increase confidence in misconceptions; confusion-then-
resolution produces learning and engagement. The hook is a well-chosen wrong belief, not a
fact.
Re-hook schedule (long-form)
● 0:15 — payoff claim landed
● 0:60-1:30 — first re-hook: escalate the promise, tease first payoff's proximity
● Every 60-90s — pattern interrupt or micro-hook
● ~3:00 — the MrBeast re-engagement moment
● Midpoint — second-biggest payoff + fresh loop opened just before it
4. BODY ARCHITECTURE
4.1 The loop engine (George Blackman's core method)
● Body = 3-5 segments (10 min) or 5-8 segments (20 min), each a Setup Tension→ →
Payoff loop. A 10-15 min video holds 5-7 complete loops.
● Write the payoffs first. Map every payoff before any setup.
● Transitions are micro-hooks. Tease the next segment BEFORE closing the current
one ("open loop across the cut").
● Never leave a loop unpaid — unpaid loops destroy trust. Payoffs need to be satisfying,
not huge.
● "Make them feel smart": show the phenomenon before the explanation (evidence
explanation, not claim evidence).→ →
4.2 The retention curve's three danger zones
1. 0-30s cliff: slow intro or packaging mismatch. Normal 15% loss; steepest at 10-≤
20s.
2. Midpoint sag (40-60% of runtime): intro energy exhausted, climax not near. Fix:
schedule the second-strongest payoff at midpoint + open a fresh loop right before it.
3. End cliff: viewers bail the moment they hear wrap-up language.
Reading the graph: flat = great; smooth decline = normal; sharp dip = one boring/confusing
moment (cut it); spikes = rewatched moments (make more of that). Compare to your channel
median, not absolutes.
4.3 Storytelling frameworks
● But/Therefore (South Park rule): audit the outline by literally writing the connective
between every pair of sections. "And then" = list of events, not a story.
● Hero's Journey lite: ordinary world + call + doubt escalating obstacles + ordeal→
transformation + lesson. Best for challenges, vlogs, case studies.→
● Question-stacking (Veritasium): Q1's answer raises Q2, Q2's raises Q3; final answer
resolves the original mystery at a deeper level. Each answer is a payoff AND a new
setup.
● Curiosity payoff scheduling: small payoff by 60-90s · medium every 2-3 min · second-
biggest at midpoint · biggest at 80-90%. Never front-load or back-load all value.
● Visual-anchor alternation (Johnny Harris): anchor context/explanation new→ →
anchor, cycling for 15-30 min. Script supports the visuals, not the reverse.
Personalize stakes early.

6. SCRIPTING MECHANICS
6.1 Word math
● Delivery clusters: education/storytelling 130-140 wpm · standard 145-160 · fast
tech/commentary 170-180.
● Formula: minutes × wpm × 0.85 = script word count (0.85 covers pauses,
B-roll-only beats, demos).
● Calibrate personally: read 300 words aloud, time it.
6.2 Full script vs. bullets
● Full script: essays, documentaries, VO-heavy, ramblers, non-native speakers,
anything edited word-by-word. Blackman's 4-Hat method: Artist (brainstorm) →
Architect (structure) Writer (connect every point to its payoff) Wizard→ →
(retention pass).
● Bullets: personality-led talking heads, screen-recording tutorials, vlogs.
● The modern default is hybrid: script the seams, riff the segments. Word-for-word:
the hook, the first 60 seconds, every transition, and the ending.
6.3 Writing for the mouth
Short sentences · contractions · second person · one idea per sentence · read every draft
ALOUD and delete anything you stumble on.
6.4 Two-column AV format
AUDIO (narration) | VIDEO (B-roll, graphics, overlays, demos). Plan every 60-90s pattern
interrupt at the WRITING stage. Solo-creator minimum: inline cues — [B-ROLL: …], [TEXT
ON SCREEN: …], [CUT TO DEMO] — at least every 15-25 lines.
6.5 Chapters
Write them into the script (they're your segment outline). Cited YouTube data: chaptered
videos ~20% higher avg watch time, ~15% higher satisfaction, ~10% lower abandonment.
Use for tutorials/listicles/info-dense; skip (or use teasing titles) for narratives — chapters
let viewers skip the build. Mechanics: first at 0:00, 3 chapters, 10s each.≥ ≥ Chapter titles
are micro-hooks, not labels: "The mistake that cost me $40K", not "Mistake #3".
7. ENDINGS — OUTROS ARE DEAD
The retention graph collapses the instant viewers hear wrap-up language, dragging AVP down
and killing the end-screen click window.
1. Never say "thanks for watching", "that's it for today", or recap-with-music.
2. Land the final payoff, then pivot immediately. Blackman's bridge: Link Curiosity→
Gap Promise→ — reference the next video, open a loop only IT closes, promise its
transformation. (Ed Lawrence: the most valuable CTA is the next video, not any external
link.)
3. End-screen mechanics (final ~20s): 1-5s final thought 5-12s verbal, SPECIFIC→
watch-next pitch (name the video out loud) 12-20s music bed with elements up.→
Use 2-3 elements, never 4. Pick the video 70% of THIS video's audience would≥
want next. Decide the watch-next target BEFORE scripting and plant a setup for it
mid-video.
4. Subscribe ask: 55-75% through runtime, right after a major payoff, worded as
viewer benefit ("If you want a breakdown like this every week, subscribe"). Reported 20-
35% better conversion vs end-of-video asks. Max 1-2 CTAs per video — every extra ask
dilutes all of them.
5. MrBeast rule: end abruptly unless the ending itself is the hype.

9. DOs & DON'Ts (named practitioners)
MrBeast: DO front-load the wow · match clickbait in minute one · plan a 3-minute re-
engagement · end abruptly. DON'T tell when you can show after minute 1 · aim for "highest
quality" — aim for the best YouTube video.
Paddy Galloway: DO overweight pre-production · write + audio-test 3-4 intros · titles <50
chars, thumb text 5 words · retention-map before scripting. DON'T start production≤
before the idea is proven.
Ed Lawrence: DO undo the clickbait in the intro · embed lessons in stories · ship (done >
perfect) · 70/20/10 planning/editing/filming. DON'T linger on one point · chase trends outside
your lane.
George Blackman: DO build hooks from Target–Transformation–Stakes · run Setup–
Tension–Payoff every 60-90s · show before explaining · end Link Curiosity Gap→ →
Promise. DON'T reveal payoffs in setups · use AI-cliché diction.
Colin & Samir: DO treat the click as act one of the story · respect viewer time from second zero
· invite the repeat view. DON'T separate "content" from "packaging".
Derek Muller: DO open with the misconception · tolerate productive confusion · teach through
story and dialogue. DON'T present clean facts and expect them to stick.
Johnny Harris: DO write visuals first · front-load a visual anchor in 30-60s · alternate
anchor/context. DON'T open with abstract context before showing something.
Jake Thomas (hook wording): DO write to curiosity, fear, desire · speak from experience ("I
regretted…") not presumption ("You will regret…").
